# How to Be the Brand AI Recommends (Similarweb)

**Author:** Darrell Mordecai, Content Marketing Manager at Similarweb.

## How AI understands brands and entities

AI organizes its knowledge by finding patterns — how entities (brands,
people, concepts) appear in relation to each other across millions of
sources — rather than by filing/indexing. Entities that consistently
appear in the same conversations get grouped together. A brand that
keeps showing up alongside "cloud security" and "enterprise software"
gets pulled into that neighborhood; one that shows up inconsistently or
in conflicting contexts stays fuzzy. The more consistent the signal and
the more sources that agree on what a brand is/stands for, the more
confidently AI can place it. "Think of it less like a database lookup
and more like building a reputation through sheer weight of evidence."

## Two data sources

AI draws from two pools when building an answer:

| | Training data ("what it knows") | Live retrieval ("what it can check") |
|---|---|---|
| Description | Frozen snapshot of the internet baked in at training time | Real-time web retrieval supplementing/verifying the model's memory |
| Update cycle | Months to years | Real time |
| Brand discovery | Only brands present before cutoff | Any brand with a crawlable presence |
| Confidence level | High — deep, consistent signal | Lower — thinner profile without training data |

Established brands benefit from both. Newer brands rely more heavily on
live retrieval, making a consistent, crawlable third-party presence
essential.

## Citations vs. brand mentions

A **mention/recommendation** is the AI saying your name. A **citation**
is the AI pointing to your content as a source of information for its
answer — your website gets the footnote. Citations come specifically
from retrieval (the AI pulled your content to back up a claim). Mentions
are messier — they can come from training data (the AI learned to
associate the brand with a topic over time) or from live retrieval
(showing up in the right places at the right moment).

The difference is confidence: training data builds a rich, established
picture; live retrieval gives a thinner, current snapshot. A brand that
shows up in both carries significantly more weight. Practical stakes:
**citations drive traffic, mentions build presence.**

**Recommendation signals** (how confident is the AI that a brand belongs
in this conversation):
- Brand name appears frequently in the same topical neighborhood as its
  category
- High branded search volume — humans searching for the brand teaches
  AI that it matters
- Presence in core knowledge sources: Wikipedia, Wikidata, major
  publications
- Consistent, positive co-mentions with the category across the web

**Citation signals** (does content give AI something worth quoting):
- Unique data points not found elsewhere — information gain, not
  repetition
- Fresh content — recency is a major retrieval weight in RAG systems
- Clear structure: headers, bullets, schema markup, easy for AI to
  extract
- Co-citation: multiple independent sources pointing to the brand as the
  answer

## Why focus on brand mentions

Similarweb data shows brand mentions growing much faster than referral
traffic from AI, and the gap keeps widening (indexed growth chart, US
desktop+mobile, visits to ChatGPT/Gemini/Claude vs. referrals from AI,
2025-01 to 2026-04). AI-driven clicks still happen and are often
high-intent, but most AI interactions end without one. Every time AI
recommends a brand in response to a commercial/transactional prompt, it
reaches a buyer whether they click or not — mentions deserve a seat as a
core AEO metric, not just a fallback when citations fall short.

Quote (Director of SEO & AI Search at Similarweb): "Citations tell you
how much AI trusts your content. Brand mentions tell you whether AI
knows you exist. Since buyers are making decisions inside the AI
response, before they ever click, being mentioned is the metric that
matters first."

## Building your brand presence: the two-part strategy

Winning in AI comes down to two things:
1. **Being understood** — an on-page problem: build a website that
   gives AI systems a clear, consistent, unambiguous picture of what the
   brand does and what topic space it owns.
2. **Being trusted** — an off-page problem: earn consistent mentions
   across the third-party sources AI already looks to for validation
   (review sites, publications, communities, industry discussions).

The two work in sequence — you can't skip to step two, but step one
alone won't get you there either. Brands winning in AI have both a
tightly structured topical presence on their own site and a growing
constellation of third-party signals pointing back at them.

## Part 1: Being understood — build your topical home base (on-page)

Segment content into topics and subtopics so AI can understand a site
quickly. When a user queries an AI platform, the AI breaks the query
into sub-queries and searches for answers across all of them
simultaneously. A brand built around a clear topic with well-developed
subtopic silos surfaces consistently across those sub-queries; a
scattered, unfocused site surfaces once, maybe, and disappears.

**Quote (Aleyda Solis, Consultant/Speaker/Author, Founder of Orainti)**:
"Brands should build structured prompt sets that reflect real buyer
journeys by defining representative prompts across key topical hubs,
product categories, and stages of the funnel, from early research to
final transaction. Instead of asking 'Are we showing up for this one
query?', the better question is 'Are we consistently visible across
this intent category?' The goal is not perfection on one query, but
strong share of presence across a meaningful cluster, such as being
cited in 70 to 80 percent of relevant, high-intent prompts. AI
visibility should be measured as sustained topical authority, not
isolated wins."

**Why content structure matters**: a structured site (clear topic +
subtopic silos) surfaces across all sub-queries, builds a confident AI
entity profile, and sends a consistent topical signal. A scattered site
(unfocused, loosely related pages) surfaces in only 1-2 sub-queries, its
AI profile stays fuzzy, and it sends weak/conflicting signals.

**What AI needs from content**:
- **Answer-first structure** — lead with the answer, not the preamble;
  44% of AI citations come from the first 30% of a page.
- **Descriptive headings** — H2s/H3s that stand alone as questions or
  answers; AI uses headings to navigate and extract sections.
- **Topical completeness** — cover chosen topics and subtopics
  thoroughly; gaps in coverage mean gaps in the topical map.
- **Consistent entity language** — use the same brand name, product
  names, and topic language across every page; consistency = confidence.

**Closing topical gaps**: once content is structured into topical
clusters, identify gaps vs. competitors — manually (analyze recurring
subtopics/themes competitors cover better) or via a tool (Similarweb's
Recommendations Agent analyzes AI visibility, citation performance, and
the broader topic conversation landscape to surface the highest-impact
content actions).

## Part 2: Being trusted — building a consensus engine (off-page)

The AI's picture of a brand isn't built from what the brand says about
itself — it's built from what everyone else says about it. Every
third-party source that mentions a brand (review site, industry
publication, comparison article) gives AI context about who it is, what
it does, and what topic space it belongs in. A brand consistently
mentioned alongside "SEO tools" and "keyword research" gets mapped into
that neighborhood; a brand with scattered, inconsistent mentions stays
fuzzy or gets categorized incorrectly. Each co-citation gives more
context, helping AI understand brand identity.

**Track topic-level mention share**: use a tool to see mention share
across each topic a brand competes in, and find topics where the brand
is underrepresented relative to competitors. Example (illustrative,
Similarweb tool demo): tracking HubSpot's topical coverage shows
strongest performance across Sales (32.21%), Marketing (31.72%), and CRM
(30.29%) — all still in the ~30% range, an opportunity for further
growth — versus Automation (11.31%), Software (8.84%), and Analytics
(6.01%).

**Building topical brand mentions**: zoom into the specific pages AI
platforms consistently rely on as sources within a target topic — these
are the exact places a brand needs to show up, because being featured
there trains AI to understand the brand belongs in that topic's
conversation.

## Making your brand unforgettable

Beyond being understood and trusted, the final differentiator is
distinctiveness: what unique value, perspective, or story does the brand
bring to the conversation? In a landscape where many brands may be
"understood," a unique voice is what makes a brand memorable — being
remembered is "the ultimate win."
