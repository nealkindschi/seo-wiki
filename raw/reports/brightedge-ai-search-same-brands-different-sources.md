# Why AI Engines Cite Different Sources but Recommend the Same Brands

Source: https://www.brightedge.com/resources/weekly-ai-search-insights/ai-search-same-brands-different-sources
Organization: BrightEdge
Published: 2026-04-24
Fetched: 2026-07-07

## Core Argument

BrightEdge analyzed five AI search engines using their AI Catalyst tool
and found a paradox: engines pull from dramatically different source
types, yet consistently recommend similar brands. Implication:
marketers need unified strategies organized by source *layers* rather
than separate playbooks per engine.

## Methodology

- Engines: ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Mode, Google AI
  Overviews.
- Industries: B2B technology, education, entertainment, finance,
  healthcare, insurance, restaurants, travel, ecommerce.
- Analysis: citation share by engine, source classification, brand
  mention tracking, cross-engine overlap analysis, TLD distribution,
  sentiment classification.
- Each cited domain categorized by source type (authority, commercial/
  editorial, UGC, other). Brand mentions classified by share of voice,
  position, sentiment. Pairwise top-100 lists compared via Jaccard
  similarity. Citation artifacts from SERP disclaimers removed from
  Google surfaces.

## Key Findings

### Source divergence is dramatic
Authority share vs. UGC share by engine:
- Gemini: 26% authority, 0.2% UGC (130:1 ratio)
- Perplexity: 22% authority, 1.5% UGC
- ChatGPT: 18% authority, 0.5% UGC
- Google AI Mode: 14% authority, 7% UGC
- Google AI Overviews: 10% authority, 18% UGC (UGC outweighs authority)

Citation overlap between engines (pairwise top-100 source lists) ranges
16%-59% — a 43-point spread, i.e. inconsistent agreement on *which
sources* to cite.

### Brand convergence despite source divergence
Top-100 *brand* overlap between engines ranges 36%-55% — a 19-point
spread, much narrower than the source-overlap spread — and this
pattern holds across all pairwise engine comparisons. Different
engines cite different specific sources but land on largely the same
brands.

### Engine personality profiles
- **Gemini — Formal institutional recommender**: highest authority bias
  (26% combined gov/academic/institutional), minimal UGC (0.2%),
  highest .gov (13%) and .org (23%) share, longer/more inclusive brand
  lists.
- **ChatGPT — Long-tail editorial engine**: flattest source
  distribution of all engines (top 10 domains = only 18.5% of
  citations), near-zero UGC (0.5%), reads as a formal editorial
  assistant with a diverse source tail.
- **Perplexity — Research librarian**: concentrates on institutional
  medical/government/encyclopedic sources (30% combined), highest .edu
  share (3.2%), highest international ccTLD share (4.4%), names brands
  earliest — 86% land in position 5 or earlier.
- **Google AI Mode — Broad commercial aggregator**: widest unique
  domain catalog, most even distribution across source types, balanced
  mix of review aggregators/finance/news media, moderate UGC (7%).
- **Google AI Overviews — UGC-first engine**: ~17.5% from UGC platforms
  (35x higher than ChatGPT); a single video platform alone = 10.6% of
  citations, a single forum = 2.9%; authority sources only 9.5%.

### Critical distinction within Google products
AI Mode and AI Overviews (both Google-search-embedded) share 59%
citation overlap with each other, but Gemini behaves differently from
both — Gemini's overlap with ChatGPT (39%) exceeds its overlap with AI
Overviews (34%) or AI Mode (27%). "Google AI" is not monolithic.

### Sentiment consistency
Brand sentiment skews positive across all engines: Gemini 96%
positive/0.3% negative; ChatGPT 94%/~0%; Perplexity ~78%/11% neutral;
AI Mode 93%/1.7%; AI Overviews 89%/2.1%. Negative mentions are marginal
everywhere.

### Three-layer source framework
All engines pull from the same three layers, differing only in
weighting:
1. **Authority Layer** — government, academic, major industry
   institutions, trade associations, analyst firms, standards bodies,
   professional associations.
2. **Commercial and Editorial Layer** — review sites, comparison
   content, trade press, news media, finance data, retailer listings.
   The largest layer everywhere: 37-51% of citations across engines.
3. **User-Generated Content** — video platforms, forums, community
   sites, social networks, creator coverage.

## Strategic Recommendations

1. **Apply a three-layer strategy across all engines** — coverage
   across authority/commercial-editorial/UGC, weighted by engine
   priority, maintains visibility across all five engines rather than
   requiring five separate playbooks.
2. **Redefine "authority" by category** — authority is category-
   relative, not universal; identify which authoritative sources (trade
   associations, analyst firms, vertical experts) actually dominate
   your specific category's AI citations rather than assuming .gov/.edu
   applies uniformly.
3. **Prioritize commercial and editorial presence** — the widest
   visibility lever, the largest citation share (37-51%) across every
   engine; PR, trade coverage, review-site visibility, and comparison
   content translate across all engines.
4. **Treat UGC as non-negotiable for Google AI Overviews specifically**
   — only 18% of its citations but its defining characteristic;
   understand which specific videos/forum threads/community
   discussions it cites and earn authority within those conversations.
5. **Weight investment by engine buyer reliance** — B2B SaaS brands
   whose buyers favor ChatGPT/Perplexity should emphasize authority +
   commercial coverage with supplemental UGC; consumer brands relying
   on AI Overviews should prioritize UGC + commercial presence with
   authority reinforcement.
6. **Measure engine-specific visibility, not aggregate** — track
   overlap between Google's search-embedded surfaces (59% similarity
   suggests visibility gains transfer between AI Mode/AI Overviews)
   while recognizing Gemini behaves distinctly from both.
7. **Map citations and positions** — Perplexity's early brand
   positioning (86% in position 5 or earlier) vs. other engines'
   later mentions suggests different engagement patterns warrant
   position-aware measurement.

## Conclusions

Challenges the assumption that each AI engine needs an entirely
distinct strategy. Engines show materially different sourcing
behaviors, but brand recommendations remain surprisingly consistent.
Strategic implication: organize visibility efforts around the three
content layers all engines use, calibrated by which engines matter
most to your audience, rather than five separate playbooks. "The path
varies; the destination clusters."
