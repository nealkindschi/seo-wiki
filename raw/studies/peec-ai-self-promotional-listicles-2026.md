# Self-Promotional Listicles Analysis: Data from 232,000 Citations

Source: https://peec.ai/blog/self-promotional-listicles-analysis-from-232k-citations
Author: Tom Wells (GEO Researcher, Peec AI)
Published: 2026-07-07 (underlying research dated 2026-03-03)
Fetched: 2026-07-08

## Methodology

13,000 unique listicles analyzed, 232,000 total citations tracked, over
a 12-week period (December 2025-February 2026). Six AI platforms
monitored: ChatGPT, Google AI Mode, Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot,
Google Gemini, Google AI Overviews. Focus sector: software/software
reviews. Weekly sampling of the top 1,000 performing listicles per
platform.

Detection method: a listicle counted as "self-promotional" only when a
company's own domain both published the listicle and included its own
product within it (domain-to-brand-mention matching).

## Core Finding

Self-promotional listicles are still getting cited by AI engines at a
meaningful rate, despite widespread industry warnings to avoid them:
roughly 1 in 10 citations (~11%) in the studied sector come from
self-promotional listicles, with no evidence of algorithmic correction
over the 12-week study window.

## Platform-Specific Rates

- ChatGPT: ~3.6-4% self-promo citation rate — lowest of all platforms
  studied, roughly 3x better (lower) than Google AI Mode or Perplexity.
- Google AI Mode: ~10.3%.
- Perplexity: ~10.4%.

## Temporal Trend

- December-January: highest rates (11-14%).
- February: slight improvement for Google AI Mode (9-10%).
- Perplexity: stayed consistently elevated (10-12%) throughout.
- ChatGPT: stayed stable and low (3-5%) throughout.
- No overall downward trend detected — self-promotion rates held
  roughly stable across the study period rather than declining as
  platforms iterate on filtering.

## Why ChatGPT Differs

Three contributing factors identified:
1. More diverse source selection overall (less concentration on any
   single domain type).
2. Heavier reliance on educational/training sites (7.8% of its
   citation mix vs. 2.5-3.6% for competing platforms).
3. Much lower reliance on Zapier's own content specifically (~1% of
   citations vs. ~8% for other platforms) — Zapier is called out as a
   notable case of self-promotional content getting cited elsewhere.

## Conclusion

AI search has not yet solved the self-promotion problem — self-
promotional listicles are passing through retrieval filters and
generating real citation share despite reputational risk (user
skepticism toward obviously self-interested content) and despite the
availability of better alternatives (third-party listicles, authentic
review relationships, educational content). The finding is framed as a
current-state gap that could close as platforms iterate on filtering,
not a stable permanent loophole.
