Steven van Vessum, "Content Pruning" (Conductor Academy, last updated 2026-03-24).

## Key takeaways

- **Content pruning** = removing/fixing obsolete or low-performing
  content dragging down overall site quality, link-authority
  distribution, UX, and crawl budget (crawl-budget benefit is most
  relevant at 10,000+ pages).
- **Cadence**: continuous/staged, not an annual overhaul — every 6
  months for sites up to 1,000 pages, every 3 months for larger sites,
  with monthly assessment folded into regular content work.
- **3-step process**: full content inventory (CMS export + analytics +
  GSC/Bing Webmaster Tools + backlink data, documenting each item's
  goal/audience/target keyword) → audit against traffic, organic
  traffic, internal/external link counts, social performance,
  staleness, thinness, and cannibalization → flag and decide fate.
- **Four alternatives to deletion**, ranked from lightest touch:
  improve (refresh metadata/headings, update facts, cut stale
  sections) → trim/consolidate/merge weak pages into one stronger
  page → make non-indexable (`noindex` low-SEO-value-but-useful pages
  like blog tag pages; canonicalize duplicate PDFs) → remove, but only
  staged with 1-3 week monitoring windows between batches and pages
  bucketed by severity (traffic/backlinks/engagement).
- **Redirect discipline**: multiple practitioners (Doherty, Daly) stress
  that 301 redirects must be set carefully when removing/merging pages —
  sloppy redirect handling is called out as a common source of lasting
  traffic loss.
- Ties directly to the wiki's existing cannibalization guidance
  ([[keyword-mapping-and-cannibalization]]) — cannibalization is one of
  the audit-stage flags here, and "merge weak pages into a single
  stronger page" is the same consolidation tactic that playbook
  recommends for cannibalized keywords.
