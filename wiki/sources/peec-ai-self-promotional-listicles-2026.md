# Self-Promotional Listicles Analysis (Peec AI)

**Citation:** Wells, Tom. "Self-promotional listicles analysis: Data
from 232,000 citations." Peec AI Blog. Published 2026-07-07 (research
dated 2026-03-03).
https://peec.ai/blog/self-promotional-listicles-analysis-from-232k-citations
(Methodology: 13,000 unique listicles, 232,000 citations tracked,
12-week window Dec 2025-Feb 2026, six platforms — ChatGPT, Google AI
Mode, Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot, Google Gemini, Google AI
Overviews — software/software-reviews sector, top 1,000 listicles per
platform weekly.)

## Key takeaways

- Roughly **1 in 10 citations (~11%)** in the software sector come
  from self-promotional listicles (a company's own domain listing its
  own product) — despite common industry advice to avoid this tactic.
- **Sharp platform divergence**: ChatGPT ~3.6-4% (lowest, ~3x better
  than the worst offenders), Google AI Mode ~10.3%, Perplexity
  ~10.4%.
- **No algorithmic correction observed** over the 12-week study window
  — rates held roughly stable rather than declining, suggesting
  current filtering is inconsistent rather than actively closing the
  gap.
- ChatGPT's lower rate attributed to three factors: more diverse
  overall source selection, heavier reliance on educational/training
  sites (7.8% vs. 2.5-3.6% for competitors), and much lower reliance
  on Zapier's own content specifically (~1% vs. ~8% for other
  platforms).
- Framed as a current-state retrieval-filter gap, not a stable
  loophole — still recommends against self-promotional listicles given
  reputational risk and better alternatives (third-party listicles,
  authentic reviews, educational content).

## Conflict handling note

This finding sits in tension with (but doesn't strictly contradict)
[[e-e-a-t-and-page-quality]]'s guidance to disclose conflicts of
interest (e.g. a manufacturer's own "review" of its product) — that
page treats undisclosed self-interest as a trust/quality risk, while
this source shows such content is nonetheless being cited at a
non-trivial rate on several platforms. Not logged as a formal
Conflicting Evidence entry since the two aren't making competing
factual claims — one is a stated best practice (disclose or avoid
self-interest), the other is empirical evidence that current AI
retrieval filtering is imperfect and doesn't reliably enforce that
practice. Noted inline in both playbook and concept updates rather
than as a resolved/unresolved conflict block.
